# LinkedIn — "If It's Not the Price of Fruit"
*(redrafted 2026-06-18 from the final essay; calibrated hedged voice; no "Sage"; published 2026-06-18)*

## Post — RE-POST ⭐ (depth-first hook, measurement spine — recommended)

*v1 underperformed (~250 impressions, ~4 reactions): distribution throttled at the test batch because the soft hook ("USDA says 23%…") killed early dwell, and the profound payload sat below the fold. This version leads with the cliff + indicts measurement up top, surfaces the method early, and closes on the epistemic question.*

6 in 10 American toddlers eat enough fruit. About 6 in 100 teenagers do.

You won't see that collapse in the number everyone quotes — because of how we chose to measure it.

That number — ~23% of U.S. kids meet the fruit guideline — is right. I reproduced it three ways, including a small-area method borrowed from political science (the kind used to estimate opinion in all 50 states from one national poll), still rare in nutrition.

But it's an average. And averaging is a choice that erases the cliff: ~61% at age 2–3, down to ~6% by the late teens.

The reflexive read is money — a serious one; healthy diets cost more. But for fruit, in kids, income is a second-order lever. The age gap is ~55 points; income, ~7. The highest-adherence kids in the country aren't the richest — they're Mexican-American kids (~31%), ahead of higher-income non-Hispanic White kids (~18%).

So notice what happened. The lever everyone argues about — cost — is the small one. The biggest factor, by roughly 8×, is the one the headline averages away.

And it isn't even a number we treat as social. "Age" reads as biology — nothing in a teenager's physiology rejects an apple, but the decline gets filed as natural, the way kids "just grow out of" things. What actually changes looks social: who controls the plate, what's in the vending machine, what the market sells.

Which is where measurement does its quietest work. There are two mainstream ways to handle age, and both delete this. Geroscience measures it as biology, to the decimal. Inequality research does the opposite — adjusting age out so the "real" social variables can show. Neither is looking for a social gradient living inside age. So the single largest, most fixable signal in the data — a built environment stripping fruit away as kids gain autonomy — never makes the list of problems at all.

That's the thing about how we measure: it decides what counts as a problem. And here, the conventional choices delete the biggest one, then leave us debating the leftovers.

The data is honest. It just answers the question the instrument was built to ask — and we rarely vote on the instrument.

Which makes me wonder how much of what we call "the science" is really a picture of the choices we made before we looked.

## Post — v1 (posted 2026-06-18 · ~250 impressions, ~4 reactions)

USDA says about 23% of American children get enough fruit. The number looks right — I reproduced it a few different ways.

It may also be close to useless if you're trying to get more kids to eat fruit. "23% of children" is an average of a population where roughly 6 in 10 toddlers clear the bar and about 6 in 100 teenagers do.

🔹 Age 2–3: ~61%
🔹 Age 9–15: ~15%
🔹 Age 16–19: ~6%

The reflexive explanation for that gap is cost, and it's a serious one — a widely cited meta-analysis put the healthiest diets at about $1.48 a day more, and affordability anchors much of the ultra-processed-food debate. But for fruit, in kids, it looks like a second-order lever. The age gap is ~55 points; income is ~7. (USDA's own report finds household income and fruit prices matter *less* than behaviors signaling concern for health.) And the highest-adherence kids in the country aren't the richest — they're Mexican-American kids (~31%), ahead of non-Hispanic White kids (~18%).

👉 So if it isn't price, is it age? Maybe — but "age" isn't really an answer. Nothing in a teenager's physiology rejects an apple; what changes looks social — who controls the plate, what's in the vending machine, what the market is selling. The cliff may be a social transition wearing a biological variable's clothing.

Which makes how we usually treat age a little strange. Much of geroscience measures it to the decimal — epigenetic clocks, biological-age scores. A lot of health-inequality research does closer to the opposite, adjusting age *out* so the "real" social variables can show through. Different instincts, arguably a shared one: age as biology. The fruit cliff is harder to file under either — a 𝘀𝗼𝗰𝗶𝗮𝗹 𝗴𝗿𝗮𝗱𝗶𝗲𝗻𝘁 one tradition over-measures and the other adjusts away, and maybe the steepest one in the picture.

That seems like a variable to study, not to adjust away.
